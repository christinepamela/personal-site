Innovation needs Rebels

Elliot Kane sat at the head of the massive oak conference table, flanked by his senior leadership team. The boardroom smelled of old leather and stale coffee. The table, polished to a dull shine from years of board meetings and intense deliberations. Floor-to-ceiling windows framed the city skyline, yet no one was looking outside.

His hands were clasped. His eyes fixed on the screen behind him. The sales reports glowed red. Losses. Month after month, the numbers kept slipping.

Blenders. Washing machines. Fridges...

(Now if you are wondering why this book on innovation starts with a blender. It's a fair question. What was considered revolutionary yesterday, bringing in billions year after year, is now basic. And in today's fast-paced world, even the most mundane products can be reimagined with the right technological infusion.

Back to the meeting room...)

"We can't keep pretending this isn't happening. The market is changing, and we're not changing with it."

No one responded at first. Then heads turned toward Greg, the head of product development. He exhaled, shaking his head. "Elliot, we've been here before. We've launched eco-friendly packaging, smart labels, even partnered with influencers. None of it moved the needle. What makes you think another 'reinvention' will?"

The Culture that needs to be broken

I grew up hearing the word "culture" all the time. It was framed as a way of life, a script to follow. It told us how to behave, what to believe, and, most importantly, not to question what we were taught. Facts and truth are often blurred. Facts are objective. Truth is personal based on your interpretation. And culture confuses them both.

To me the word "culture" is arguably the most misunderstood word in both business and in life. Culture is not something you set once and forget. It's alive. It morphs. It's like mercury, fluid or solid depending on the environment. Culture is everywhere.

Most business books talk about how to "build a great company culture," but few explore what culture really is, when it is formed, how it evolves, why it holds power, and most importantly, when it needs to be broken.

Why does culture need to be broken?

When culture becomes rigid, when legacy is forced into the now without reflection, something starts to crack.

History proves this time and again.

The fall of the Western Roman Empire is a case study in cultural stagnation. At its peak under Marcus Aurelius, Rome was a force of engineering, law, and order. By the fifth century, it crumbled. Not because of one failure, but because the systems stopped evolving. What once made them powerful eventually made them vulnerable. Culture froze and so did progress.

Even Jesus challenged the culture of his time. In a patriarchal world, he spoke to women directly, treated them with dignity and gave them equal standing among his followers. He defied strict religious customs of his time. He not only preached love; he embodied rebellion against spiritual decay.

And you don't just see this in ancient history. Every era has its breakers. And these people do not wait for permission to change the system.

Nina Simone trained to be the first black female concert pianist in America. But when the Curtis Institute rejected her, likely due to racial bias, she did not give up. She did not retreat. Instead, she turned to smoky clubs, channeling rage and soul into a new sound. Her music became a battle cry for civil rights. Today, she has left a permanent mark in history as one of the most iconic and influential figures in American music, culture, and civil rights.

In 1970s London, fashion belonged to polite society. Then came Vivienne Westwood. She didn't just design clothes, she made rebellion wearable. She opened a store called SEX and dressed the Sex Pistols in ripped shirts, safety pins, and anarchic slogans. Her clothes shocked the establishment, and that was the point. Westwood's rebellion ignited underground music scenes, launched DIY culture, and inspired makers' culture. Fashion wasn't just fashion anymore. It was a revolution.

Srinivasa Ramanujan, a self-taught genius from colonial India, sent pages of raw theorems to Cambridge. He had no degree, no training, just a torrent of intuition. He said they came to him in dreams. Scholars dismissed him at first. Too messy, too mystical. But his wild formulas would go on to shape number theory and later ripple into modern physics, and even string theory. Today, modern neuroscience is just beginning to catch up, acknowledging what he already knew: the subconscious is a source of profound insight.

And in sports, in 1967, women weren't allowed to run the Boston Marathon. So Katherine Switzer entered under "K.V. Switzer." When officials tried to shove her off the course, she kept running. Cameras caught the scuffle, and history was changed forever. That single act sparked a shift in global athletics. Her defiance led to women's inclusion in marathons, rewriting the rules of sport and gender.

Each of them stood at the edge of a system that told them "no". But instead of shrinking, they shoved the boundary outward. And the world stretched with them. Music changed. Fashion changed. Science, sports, law, they all stretched because someone refused to obey, someone cared enough to confront the status quo.

These history makers were not rebels for rebellion's sake. They were builders and reformers. They carried the culture forward, by breaking it open.

And that's the point.

Culture defines the boundaries of possibility. And when those boundaries become prison walls, it's time to ask if we are preserving something meaningful, or are we just repeating a script that no longer serves us?

Every company has a moment of reckoning. The moment where the metrics are fine, but the energy is gone. The work gets delivered, but the wonder is missing. People feel like passengers instead of pioneers.

That's when you know.

The culture that built the past can't carry the future.

So what do you do then?

Do you break? Do you bend? Do you bounce?

Or do you build a new path?

The Corporate Culture Trap

As I spoke to more people within Elliot’s company, I noticed that at every level, people saw innovation differently.

The Engineers.

Sterling’s engineers were problem-solvers. They could fix anything, but they rarely questioned whether the problem itself was worth solving. To them, innovation meant making the washing machine quieter or improving the cleaning spray’s formula.

“We’re not entrepreneurs,” one engineer said during a product review. “We solve problems and make practical improvements.”

It wasn’t the first time I’d heard this sentiment in a large company. Engineers like these were not resistant to innovation, but they were constrained by their environment. At Sterling, their KPIs were tied to production yield, which meant minimizing failure and optimizing processes. These goals directly conflicted with the messy, iterative nature of high-risk innovation.

This issue wasn’t unique to Sterling. Early in my career, I worked at a company with thousands of employees generating billions in revenue. From day one, you go through rigorous business training to learn exactly what to do. Large companies need consistent and predictable execution, operating within an acceptable margin of error.

Whether you are a new or experienced employee, your fundamental goal is to master these processes. The better you execute them, the higher your rewards.

But herein lies the problem. Processes only lift you from ground zero to a bare minimum. This is the level required to be considered sufficiently competent, not extraordinary.

Yet every company has someone who wants to challenge the process, someone who sees the need for change but is always sidelined. When rules begin to hold back the best of engineering capabilities, we need to step in and adapt. How can new ideas thrive if people are stuck in the same routine day in and day out?

At Sterling, there was no larger vision to guide innovation. No spark of creativity and curiosity filtered down to the engineers on the ground.

As I tried to understand why, I asked the lead engineer, “Have you spoken to customers?”

He shook his head. “Not directly,” he admitted. “We get feedback from customer support, marketing, and other teams, but each division has different expectations. It takes time just to coordinate meetings. It is not a flat hierarchy like a startup.”

That was the other missing piece…

The Customer

It is difficult to innovate for customers if you are not speaking to them. Processes like Agile and Lean, along with cross-functional teams, try to bridge this gap. But without direct, back-and-forth conversations, engineers are left playing a game of telephone. Each layer dilutes the message further.

Even then, Sterling was only listening to its mainstream customers, the ones who already loved its current products.

When it comes to innovation and next-generation breakthroughs, mainstream customers are not the ones leading the charge. Early adopters and niche markets take that role. They experiment and refine these prototypes long before they reach mass adoption.

The Executive Disconnect

Meanwhile, at the top of the organization…

Innovation was a catchword, an axiom. Executives loved talking about it. It looked great on PowerPoint slides and media. But when truly game-changing ideas surfaced, ones that could disrupt core business lines, panic sets in.

Over the years I’ve come to realize that people love to talk about embracing change, but rarely act on it.

For thousands of years, Zen Buddhism, Stoicism, Hinduism, Christianity, Sufism, and Existentialism have all preached the same thing. Adapt. Let go. Evolve. We nod along as if it's second nature. When Bruce Lee says, be like water, my friend, we hail it. But when faced with real change, we slam the brakes so hard you'd think we never saw it coming.

“Why not just acquire a startup?” one VP suggested during a leadership meeting. “It’s easier than trying to innovate internally.”

This logic wasn’t unique to Sterling. Large companies often see acquisitions and startup investments as a shortcut to innovation. Why waste the money and headache trying to create magic internally when you can just buy one? On the surface, it looks simple, like a day out shopping. But the truth is, almost 80-90% of acquisitions fail. Cultural integration, timing of market capture, performance, and cross-functional knowledge always come into play.

In the late 90s, General Motors (GM) were leading the electric vehicles race. It launched the EV1.

But in 2003, GM killed it. Why? They cited high production costs and limited demand. Some argue that pressure from oil companies and dealership networks also played a role, along with GM’s preference for more profitable gas-powered vehicles. Whatever the reason, the decision cost GM its early advantage.

By the time Tesla emerged with the Roadster in 2008, the EV market was back in motion, but without GM leading it. The EV1 could have positioned GM as the dominant player in electric cars. Instead, it abandoned the space and allowed Tesla to take the lead.

GM has since tried to catch up through acquisitions and partnerships, but it has never matched Tesla’s innovation or market leadership. It bought stakes in EV startups, invested in battery technology, and even partnered with Honda to co-develop electric and hydrogen fuel cell vehicles. In 2020, GM announced a $2 billion investment in Nikola, a hydrogen and electric truck startup, only for the deal to unravel after fraud allegations against Nikola’s founder.

Even its internal efforts have struggled to gain traction. The Chevy Bolt was a solid attempt, but it never captured the cultural momentum of Tesla’s vehicles. Meanwhile, Tesla kept pushing boundaries by expanding its charging network, vertically integrating battery production, and launching innovations like over-the-air software updates that GM still lags behind on.

You can acquire technology. But you cannot buy vision.

Elliot saw the same risk at Sterling. “We’ve acquired startups before,” he told me, “But they either don’t scale, or we smother them with our processes.” Acquisitions weren’t fixing the deeper problem. Sterling lacked vision and a true innovation culture.

They were afraid to build breakthrough ideas in-house, nurture them, and merge them back into the company as a core business. It was messy and uncertain.

But the real problem was, no one really knew how to do it.

Large businesses excel at budget cuts, short-term targets, and incremental updates. Startups specialize in ideas that seem crazy at first. “Why take risks when you can just buy them out to do the hard work?” But Elliot knew better. Acquisitions might fix a gap. If you're lucky, they might bring in fresh people. But they don’t transform a company.

Not unless you’re willing to transform the culture first.
